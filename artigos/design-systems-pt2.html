Colour

A fundamental building block in your design system is colour. Colour is everywhere and will persist in everything you’ll build. From outlines to backgrounds and components, these are made visible to the eye through the use of colour.

Things to consider…

Nathan Curtis has 16 Tips for Setting Up a System That Endures in reference to colour and goes into detail on each point. I’ve highlighted seven of them here worth noting.

  1. Stabilise Brand Colours Quickly— Decide your essential brand colours early, because they spread widely, quickly.
  2. Involve Brand (If You Alter a Brand Colour)— Brand colours are the brand team’s territory. So discuss adjustments with them and defer to their judgment as needed.
  3. Drop the Neutral Neutrals— Provide a few light grays and a few dark grays to achieve useful contrast, but don’t get wishy washy wireframey. Consider avoiding medium grays in between.
  4. Go “Digital Blue.” Everybody Else Does. — Links have always been blue, perhaps since the dawn of the first browser. When (not if ;) ) you go with your “Digital Blue,” choose an accessible one and make sure it doesn’t clash with the brand’s own blue, or red, orange, purple, or green.
  5. Define How Theming Works— Identify how theming works, particularly via reference to specific UI element properties in play. Just as important, articulate which — if not most — elements are off limits.
  6. Check Contrast Early & Ritually— Any system designer responsible for colour must be familiar with WCAG 2.0 rules, have a tool (like Tanaguru ) to test colour pairs, and incorporate the practice into colour selection.
  7. Limit Tint & Shade Quantity— Offer a handful of options and avoid tedious variety. Empower system users with just enough choice: more than a single option, but only up to a few intentional choices.

Ether have built a modular base for any design system worth checking out. When creating a comprehensive colour system, they suggest you address colour across three main uses:

  1. UI Colours— UI colours apply to the core aspects of your interface: buttons, text, and inputs. You want to make sure that you have access to the appropriate action colours, hover colours, and other interface colours needed to build your components and guide users to your products goals.
  2. Semantic Colours— Sometimes colours have to mean something. You’ll want to make sure you include colours that can be used for success, warning, danger, and other instances where using a specific colour matters more than being unique or on-brand.
  3. Layout Colours— These are the core of your palette and used for page layout: containers, borders, cards, rules, headers, footers, and sidebars.

Marcin Treder form UXPin mentions that a colour palette must be:

  1. Sufficiently precise— defines a small number of approved primary colours and a sufficient number of accent colours.
  2. Clearly named— developers and designers should easily refer to particular colours defined in the system. Colour names are easily understood, memorable, and spark meaningful conversations between designers and developers (shades changes, contrast corrections etc.).
  3. Accessible— both in terms of external accessibility (how easily all users perceive colour combos) and internal accessibility (how easily designers and developers find and use the palette).

So yes, using colour across a design system requires a great deal of consideration. If you’re using pre-defined brand colours, remember they need to be applied in a way that makes sense and is easily accessible in the two ways mentioned above.

Accessibility

Accessibility is the inclusive practice of ensuring there are no barriers that prevent interaction with, or access to, websites on the World Wide Web by people with disabilities . When sites are correctly designed, developed and edited, generally all users have equal access to information and functionality. ~ Wikipedia

But, accessibility applies to more than just the web, accessibility needs consideration across all digital products, and therefore, considered across your design system.

Colour accessibility enables people who are visually impaired or have deficiencies with colour to interact with digital products in the same way as their non-visually-impaired counterparts.

In 2017, The World Health Organisation estimated that roughly 217 million people live with some form of moderate to severe vision impairment.

“Not creating accessible products is just rude, so don’t be rude.” ~ Justin Reyna

Add enough contrast

People with color blindness are less likely to see the contrast between certain colours. There are lots of tools to help you test the accessibility of colour combinations, but the ones I’ve found most helpful are Colorable and Colorsafe which calculate the contrast ratio between colours and ensures they meet Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG)

To meet the minimum W3C AA rating , your contrast ratio should be at4.5or3for large text. So, when designing things like buttons, cards, or navigation elements, be sure to check the contrast ratio of your colour combinations.

When creating colour systems, be sure to also check the contrast ratios on light and dark backgrounds. Depending on your colour choices, you may need to develop two colour systems or a set of rules for applying colour to both light and dark interfaces.

“Seize the opportunity to advocate for accessibility. Always be probing a collaborator’s knowledge of accessibility (or lack thereof) and educate and advocate all you can.”

~ Nathan Curtis

Define your primary colours

These are often dictated by brand and are the main colours you’ll typically see used everywhere. These consist of between one and three primary colours but could contain more.

Google describes primary colours in their design system, Material, in the following way:

A primary colour is the colour displayed most frequently across your app’s screens and components.

If you don’t have a secondary colour, your primary colour can also be used to accent elements.

“Decide your essential brand colours early, because they spread widely, quickly.” ~ Nathan Curtis

Define your secondary colours

Sometimes brands extend their colours into a secondary pallet, and at times a tertiary colour set is also specified. These colours appear far less frequently, and act more like accent colours. Secondary and tertiary colour palettes are often used in the case of complex data visualisation or graphs.

Secondary colours are optional, but they provide more opportunities to distinguish your product. For example, by adding in a secondary colour, you can make your UI more interesting.

Tints, shades and colour steps

Accent colours are used to emphasise some UI elements such as buttons or progress bars, or to highlight some information. Accent colours usually have more brightness and saturation — such visual properties make UI elements pop off the screen and encourage user interactions.

Material design back in 2014 had a massive fourteen steps of each core colour. You may think this gave a nice set list of options, but instead it created a massive paradox of choice. Too much choice leads to massive inconsistencies when spreading these colours across a design system. You’d be much better served limiting to three of four tints maximum per colour. Googles new colour system tools in material design help limit the colour steps and limit the colour palette when choosing accent colours.

“Offer a handful of options and avoid tedious variety. Empower system users with just enough choice: more than a single option, but only up to a few intentional choices.”

~ Nathan Curtis

Charts

Charts are possibly the only time a larger list of colour steps should be used. Samantha Zhang at Graphiq suggests having a wide range in both hue and brightness for charts . Differences in brightness are universal. Take any monochromatic color palette and test how it looks in Protanopia, Deuteranopia, and grayscale mode . You’ll quickly be able to tell how accessible this palette is.

There are two common approaches to tackling colour steps:

  1. Shades — Adjust the opacity to derive a shade.By simply knocking back the opacity of a colour, you can change its shade. Something to be aware of when using this method, the base colour mixes with the background colour it is used on. The shades appearance will be different on a light colour compared to a dark colour due to the transparency that happens. This method could also lead to accessibility problems if it’s not carfully monitored. To combat this, you could possibly convert these shades and rather specify their colour values like #928C76. Shades also tend to create a less vibrant set of colours as they appear more dull as they get lighter. My suggestion would be to only use this method on simple interactions like a hovers and links and possibly borders.
  2. Tints — Adjusting brightness and saturation.Using this method on each of your base colours. You’ll be mimicking how colour is perceived in nature when light and shadow falls onto a colour. Erik D. Kennedy does a great job explaining how this works in his practical framework for using colour in UI. Perhaps a little more technical than adjusting colour with shades, but a much better approach, with less drawbacks and a more vibrant set of colour steps.

“Darker colour variations are made by lowering brightness and increasing saturation. Brighter color variations are made by increasing brightness and lowering saturation.” ~ Erik D. Kennedy

Greys or neutrals

Neutrals have a supportive role in UI design — these colours are usually made for text and background. Most often neutrals are represented by greys.

In Material design, instead of defining specific code for black or grey, they use the opacity of black to apply typography a value. In other words, they use the ‘shades’ approach mentioned above.

Informative colours

These colours describe the status, i.e. success, info, warning and danger. These colour choices should be based on the colour psychology of signal. Here is a brief guide on common colours that are used:

  • Red for error, danger, wrong.
  • Green for success, safe, right.
  • Yellow for alert, caution, warning.
  • Blue for information.

Naming your colours

The guys over at CodyHouse have created an awesome design systems template. Sebastiano Guerriero has documented the process and when it comes to colour, he mentions that colour is mostly about semantics. These are the three goals he suggests you aim for when working with colours in a design system:

  1. The colour variables have to beeasy to remember
  2. The system has to beeasy to update
  3. The system should includeonly the essential colours

Semantics

There are many different approaches to naming colours, but to achieve the three goals mentioned above, these are my suggestions:

1. Give your colours names— this is easer to remember when having conversations with designers, developers and project stakeholders.

Example: faded-jade = #407F7F seance = #9C27B0 san-mariano = #3D70B2 or green = #18BC9C blue =#3498DB yellow = #d4860b red = #E74C3C

2. Map colours to their function— this abstraction makes colour easier to manage for theming or if your colour pallet ever has to change.

Example: brand-primary = faded-jade (#407F7F)brand-secondary = seance(#9C27B0)brand-tertiary = san-mariano(#3D70B2)or colour-success = green(#18BC9C)colour-info = blue(#3498DB)colour-warning = yellow(#d4860b) colour-danger = red(#E74C3C)

3. Number shades and tints— in the interest of not bloating your list of colours you might be inclined to name your colour steps in the following manor: “ brand-primary-light ” or “ brand-primary-dark ”. This could cause issues should you ever need another option as mentioned earlier when generating graphs.

Nathan Curtis ’s suggestion is to scale colour names between 0 and 100 based on HSL’s lightness. The scale reflects a familiar range from dark to light, allows for injecting new options between.

Example:gray-100 = #2e2e31 gray-60 = #7b7a7d gray-40 = #a5a5a6 gray-30 = #bbbbbc gray-20 = #d1d0d2 gray-10 = #e8e7e8

It creates a simple and reusable mental model for changing stops.This is where more established color systems with lightness-based variable names falter.

Generating Colors

Ether goes into detail around about the pros and cons of how you could generate colours in code with these three methods, here’s a quick summary:

Pros:Speed; Flexibility; ConsistencyCons:Lack of Control; Color Bias

2. Hand-Picked

Pros:Control; QualityCons:Time; Change

You can get more bang for your buck by combining the above methods. This is the basic concept behind the method I suggested for naming your colours earlier.
